Reducing Food Waste

5 Simple Habits That Can Cut Household Food Waste in Half

Adan Rehan Abbasi July 8, 2026 4 min read
Share this article

Globally, nearly a third of all food produced is lost or wasted — yet hundreds of millions of people go to bed hungry every night. This contradiction sits at the heart of FreeHunger's mission. While a large share of food waste happens at the production and retail level, households play a surprisingly large role too, and the good news is that small daily habits can make a meaningful dent.

We spoke with our nutrition and logistics teams to compile five practical, low-effort habits that any household can adopt starting today. None of them require special equipment or major lifestyle changes — just a shift in how we think about the food already sitting in our kitchens.

1. Plan Meals Before You Shop

Impulse grocery shopping is one of the biggest contributors to household food waste. Before heading to the market, take ten minutes to plan meals for the week and write a list based on what you actually need. This single habit alone can reduce unnecessary purchases by a significant margin, since you're far less likely to buy items that end up forgotten in the back of the fridge.

Left: Thoughtful sourcing reduces waste before it even reaches your kitchen. Right: Surplus food rescued and redirected to families in need.

2. Understand "Best Before" vs. "Use By"

Many households throw away perfectly edible food simply because they misunderstand date labels. "Best before" indicates quality, not safety — the food is often still perfectly fine to eat after this date, just perhaps less fresh in taste or texture. "Use by," on the other hand, is a safety-related date and should be respected more strictly. Learning this distinction alone can prevent a surprising amount of unnecessary waste.

"Reducing food waste isn't about perfection — it's about awareness. Small daily choices, repeated consistently, create enormous impact over time."

3. Store Food the Right Way

Improper storage is a silent contributor to spoilage. Herbs stored upright in a glass of water last significantly longer than those left loose in the fridge drawer. Root vegetables like potatoes and onions should be kept away from each other, as one accelerates the spoiling of the other. A few minutes of correct storage can extend the life of your produce by days, sometimes weeks.

Here are a few quick habits worth adopting in your kitchen:

  • Keep a "eat me first" bin in the fridge for items nearing spoilage.
  • Freeze leftovers within two days instead of letting them sit in the fridge.
  • Repurpose vegetable scraps into broths, stocks or compost.
  • Serve smaller portions initially, allowing seconds rather than over-plating.
  • Donate unopened, non-perishable surplus to your nearest FreeHunger collection point.

4. Get Creative With Leftovers

Leftovers don't have to mean boring reheated meals. Stale bread becomes croutons or breadcrumbs. Overripe fruit becomes smoothies or jam. Vegetable peels become crispy snacks when roasted with a little oil and salt. Reframing "leftovers" as ingredients for a new dish, rather than waste waiting to happen, can transform your relationship with food entirely.

5. Donate Instead of Discard

Finally, when you genuinely have more food than you can use — whether from an event, a bulk purchase, or a restaurant closing early — consider donation before disposal. FreeHunger's food rescue network partners with households, restaurants and vendors across the country to collect safe, surplus food and redirect it to families who need it most, often within hours of collection.

Reducing food waste is one of the simplest and most immediate ways any household can contribute to the fight against hunger. It costs nothing, requires no special skills, and directly supports two goals at once: less waste, and more meals reaching the people who need them.
