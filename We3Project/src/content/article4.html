A Day in the Life of a FreeHunger Volunteer

Hina Malik July 6, 2026 6 min read
Share this article

People often ask me what it's actually like to volunteer with FreeHunger — whether it's exhausting, whether it's emotionally heavy, whether it's worth giving up a Saturday for. After eight months of volunteering almost every week, I can honestly say it is all of those things, and also one of the most rewarding routines I've built into my life. Here's what a typical volunteer day looks like, from start to finish.

6:30 AM — Arrival at the Distribution Center

My day starts early, well before the city wakes up. By 6:30 AM, our team gathers at the distribution warehouse where the previous night's food rescue deliveries are already stacked and waiting to be sorted. There's a quiet energy in the room — coffee cups, quiet conversation, and the shared understanding that everyone showed up voluntarily, before sunrise, because they wanted to be there.

The coordinator briefs us on the day's route: which neighborhoods we're covering, how many families are on the list, and any special considerations — a family with a newborn needing extra formula, an elderly resident who lives alone and needs a wellness check alongside their food package.

Left: Morning briefing and team coordination before the route begins. Right: Sorting and packing food boxes for the day's deliveries.

8:00 AM — Sorting and Packing

Packing food boxes sounds simple, but there's real skill involved. Each package is customized based on family size and dietary needs — more rice and lentils for larger households, softer foods for households with elderly members, baby formula and diapers where needed. We work in pairs, checking each box against a list before sealing it, making sure nothing is forgotten.

"The work is physical and repetitive, but it never feels mechanical. Every box represents a specific family, with a specific need, that we get to meet directly."

10:00 AM — On the Road

By mid-morning, our small convoy of motorbikes and a delivery van heads out across the assigned neighborhoods. This is usually the most emotionally intense part of the day. Some visits are quick — a smile, a thank you, a box handed over the doorstep. Others turn into longer conversations, especially with families we've been supporting for months and have gotten to know personally.

One of my favorite parts of route day is watching children run toward the van the moment they hear it approaching. It's a small moment, but it's also a reminder of exactly why this work matters — not in abstract statistics, but in specific, individual lives.

What a Typical Volunteer Shift Involves

  • Morning briefing and route assignment with the field coordinator.
  • Sorting, packing and quality-checking food boxes for accuracy.
  • Direct doorstep delivery to assigned households across the city.
  • Brief wellness checks and note-taking for families needing follow-up.
  • End-of-day debrief and inventory reconciliation back at the center.

2:00 PM — Wrapping Up

By early afternoon, most routes are complete and volunteers return to the center for a final debrief. This is where we share stories from the day, flag families who might need additional support beyond food, and log everything for the coordination team. It's tiring — my legs ache, my voice is a little hoarse from talking all day — but there's a specific kind of satisfaction that comes from knowing exactly how your morning translated into real impact for real people.

If you've been considering volunteering but aren't sure what to expect, I'll say this: you don't need any special skills to start. You need reliable shoes, a willingness to show up early, and an open heart. Everything else, the team will teach you. And I promise — the first time a child waves at your delivery van from down the street, you'll understand exactly why so many of us keep coming back.
